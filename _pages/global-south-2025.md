# Project
     
The relationships between UNIBO and Samarkand started in 2001, thanks to the Uzbek-Italian Archaeological Project (UIAP) with a collaboration between DISCI and the Samarkand Institute of Archaeology, Agency for Cultural Heritage of the Republic of Uzbekistan, and it is still ongoing thanks to the support of UNIBO ALMASCAVI. In 2017, a delegation from UNIBO was invited to attend the celebration of SamSU's 90th anniversary. Later, in 2019, UNIBO funded a Summer School at SamSU entitled “Environment and archaeology: linking the past and the future for a sustainable development of the Samarkand territory” that promoted the mobility of 11 students and 3 professors from UNIBO to SamSU, and 3 professors from SamSU to UNIBO. In 2023, DISTAL was funded within the Erasmus+ International Credit Mobility program (Key Action 1, Learning mobility for higher education students and staff) to promote the mobility of 4 Uzbekistan students, 2 PhD students, and 1 professor to UNIBO, along with 3 teaching staff members moving from UNIBO to SamSU. In 2025, an Erasmus+ Capacity Building in the Higher Education (Higher Education Modernization to boost the Uzbekistan Agricultural system and promote excellence and regional development), coordinated by Prof. Toselli, was financed. UNIBO-Samarkand ties deepened with visits to Uzbekistan by Italian President Sergio Mattarella in 2023 (with the UIAP Co-director Simone Mantellini; LINK), and the UNIBO Rector Prof. Giovanni Molari in 2024 (LINK). Since 2024, UNIBO has joined the “Knowledge Networks” project, led by University Cà Foscari and supported by Italy’s Foreign Ministry; UNIBO leads the cluster on water resource management to foster cooperation on water issues between Italy and Central Asian states. 
With respect to past projects, this project brings innovation and optimization of water resources by studying the policies of water usage and applying them to innovative solutions based on sensors and artificial intelligence. 
    
     
Although Samarkand is associated with the rich trade along the ancient Silk Roads and its Timurid monuments, the richness of this city and its oasis has been historically based on agriculture (Mantellini 20202). Due to its geographical location in a semi-arid environment, the possibility to develop agriculture and economic activities depends on the creation and management of a complex irrigation network. According to the UIAP investigation, the origin of the irrigation systems in Samarkand can be dated around the 4th-3rd centuries BCE (Mantellini 2020). Dargom, Siab, Yanghìaryk, and many other canals were dug in the southern part of the Valley to ensure water to the city of Samarkand and cultivated fields in the countryside. The ancient Samarkand oasis flourished along the banks of the Zerafshan River. This proximity to water was essential to the development of a complex network of canals allowing for settlement and cultivation on the floodplain. The present-day waterscape is therefore the result of uninterrupted water management over time (Mantellini 20153; Zinzani 2015a4, b5). Since then, regular maintenance of the canals over the centuries has made Samarkand one of the most important agricultural oases in Central Asia. Numerous carpological remains of barley, chickpea, pea, peach, apricot, cherry, and preserved whole walnuts from Kafir Kala and other archaeological excavations (Mantellini et al. 20166) highlight the uniqueness of an agricultural production that still forms the basis of the local economy. Samarkand's primary role as an agricultural oasis continued during the USSR. Starting in 1953, the five-year national development plans transformed part of the uncultivated lands into cotton cultivation (Pasdargom district) and the production of grapes (Taylak and Samarkand districts) and tobacco (Urgut district) was implemented (Mantellini and Berdimuradov 20197). At a broader scale, this overexploitation and inappropriate use of water resulted in dramatic environmental disasters, such as the Aral Sea desiccation and the salinization of vast regions of Central Asia (Saiko 19988). Even if Uzbekistan land area is mainly occupied by desert, the agricultural sector accounts for about 30% of the gross domestic product (Sankar, 20219) and it mainly focus on permanent meadow and pastures, while crops occupy less than 10% of the whole country area (FAOSTAT10). Due to high temperature in summer and low annual precipitation (300 mm) mainly concentrated in autumn and spring water is essential for growing crops. Consequently, availability of water of good quality is the top priority for agriculture in Uzbekistan. During the Soviet era, about 70% of the irrigated cropland was cultivated with cotton. The continuous cultivation of cotton and wheat without a proper rotation has produced, over the years, a soil degradation all over the country particularly in the regions of Karakalpakstan, Bukhara, Jizzakh, Navoi, Sydarya and Khorezm. These soils suffer from salinity, low organic matter, chemical pollution (related to the widespread use of herbicides and pesticides), poor biodiversity, low or negligible microbial biomass. In addition, water availability is scarce, and the supply methods are not efficient since flow irrigation is the most used technique. Moreover, water is supplied without taking into consideration soil water availability and/or plant evapotranspiration.
Over the last two decades water and land challenges have been exacerbated by the effects of climate change that have posed new challenges to water and agricultural institutions, as well as to local communities and their livelihoods. 
     
It is therefore strategic to understand the main challenges that water governance institutions are 
facing, together with grassroots knowledge and adaptation strategies of local communities, in order 
to develop international dialogue and cooperation avenues. 
In this framework, the use of probes and techniques able to optimize water supply together with the 
training of farmers could help to reduce water waste and enhance crop performances. Consequently, 
the present project has the main objective to analyze how the eco-climate crisis is shaping water 
resources and its governance in a diachronic perspective and specifically to understand challenges 
and solutions of mitigation and adaptation. Therefore, it aims first to provide innovation in Uzbek 
irrigation techniques by introducing precise system to monitor soil water content and by training 
farmers, technicians, and students to the use of these instruments also promoting mobility to Italy to 
visit fields where this strategy is adopted and to follow seminars on the topic. Second, to exchange 
knowledge, ideas and practices with members of water governance institutions at different scales, 
including representatives and water users of local communities (mahalla). Italian researcher will also 
move to Uzbekistan for farmers and technicians training as well as for classes to students.

## PROJECT OBJECTIVES

Water resources and related governance framework constitute a key angle to understand the processes 
of territorial transformation in the Samarkand region from a diachronic perspective. Over the last 
decade territorial transformations were shaped by climate change (reduction of winter snowfall, heat 
waves and variations in average annual precipitations), which were combined to land degradation and 
soils salinization issues. Therefore, it is strategic today to understand how institutions at different 
scales and local communities are dealing with these territorial transformations and uncertainties in 
water availability and are adapting to climate change effects in terms of governance reconfigurations, 
water distribution, agricultural changes and socio-spatial practices more in general. Moreover, new 
technologies for irrigation management play a key role in this process of transformation and 
international cooperation where it will be strategic to ensure sustainable agriculture and water security 
for local communities. The project aims to: 
1. analyze the heritage dimension of canalization projects and the political-social dimension of 
contemporary water governance, institutions, and local communities; 
2. explore the technical-agricultural dimension and, above all, the new technologies for 
irrigation, also in the light of the necessary adaptation to the effects of the eco-climatic crisis 
and safeguarding resources; 
3. facilitate the transfer and adoption of advanced technologies for water consumption 
optimization to the Uzbek context to enhance local sustainable water management; 
4. strengthen the institutional network between UNIBO, SamSU, and other local actors.
